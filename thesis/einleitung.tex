\chapter{Introduction}

\section{What are Normative Specifications?}

A norm is defined as a rule or standard that designates actions, decisions, or states of affairs as obligatory, permitted, or prohibited within a specific context. 

A normative system comprises a set of norms along with the relevant conditions, agents, institutions, or procedures necessary for their interpretation and application. The main goal of these systems is to apply the rules of norms they contain to guide, regulate, and evaluate behavior and states of affairs. Agents may comply with a norm or violate it, with violations generally resulting in consequences enforced by the system such as sanctions, penalties, reparations, or other corrective responses.

A normative specification is an explicit and structured representation of some of the norms associated with a normative system. Normative specifications may take the form of legal texts, technical standards, contracts, policies, requirements documents, processes, or executable rule sets. They are most often written in natural language and, more recently, increasingly defined using controlled natural language or formal language, each offering different trade-offs between expressiveness and precision. Normative specifications are typically targeted toward systems and their components, processes, or agents whose behavior can be assessed against the norms they express.

An important distinction within normative specifications may be drawn between \emph{ought-to-do} and \emph{ought-to-be} specifications. 

\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Ought-to-be (Seinsollen):} Norms describing ideal states of affairs without specifying how to achieve them. For example, a regulation stating that ``the server room temperature must remain below 20$^{\circ}$C'' defines a required outcome. Violations are defined by the system state, but attributing responsibility requires additional context.

    \item \textbf{Ought-to-do (Tunsollen):} Norms prescribing specific actions to be performed by specific agents. For example, ``the administrator must activate the cooling system if the temperature exceeds 19$^{\circ}$C.'' In this case, the focus is on agency and behavior. 

\end{itemize}

While ``ought-to-be'' specifications are common in high-level policy, operational compliance and blame assignment rely heavily on translating these goals into precise ``ought-to-do'' rules that can be monitored against agent actions.

\section{The Digitalization of Normative Systems}

Digital technology is now part of almost every area of daily life, from how we pay for things and organize our schedules to how we manage meetings and travel.
Rules and norms help guide these interactions between agents and systems. As more of these interactions are digitalized, it becomes increasingly important to find ways to represent, guide, monitor, and enforce these rules using computer programs.

\subsection{The Cost of Compliance}
The cost of compliance is the economic and practical burden that rules and regulations place on governments, businesses, and individuals. This burden is not just about the money spent on enforcement. It also includes extra paperwork, slower decisions, less access to markets, and less efficient ways of working. Studies show that in places with many regulations, these costs can make up a significant part of the economy.

For the European Union, the European Commission has estimated that administrative burdens may amount to around $3.5\%$ of GDP, based on an extrapolation approach and a specific definition of administrative burden.\footnote{Source (visited on 21 December 2025) \url{https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/HTML/?uri=CELEX\%3A52006DC0691}}

For the United States, economy-wide estimates of the aggregate annual cost of federal regulation have been reported in the trillion-dollar range in the literature, with one widely cited estimate reporting roughly \$2.0 trillion in 2012.\footnote{Sources (visited on 21 December 2025)~\url{https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R44348} \; \\  \url{https://conservativereform.com/files/data-and-reports/cost-of-federal-regulations/federal-regulation-executive-summary.pdf}}

Regulatory burden arises not only from the substantive demands of regulation, but also from its structural complexity. Normative systems are typically fragmented across jurisdictions, amended incrementally, and expressed in heterogeneous legal language. As a result, regulated entities must invest considerable effort and time to determine which duties and rights apply in a given situation and whether exceptions or loopholes apply. This analytical effort is repeated across organizations and over time, which leads to duplicated work and increases the chance of avoidable errors.

These challenges are especially clear in busy administrative tasks. Tasks like procurement, licensing, certification, and eligibility checks often require extensive manual review, even when the rules are clear. Small mistakes or misunderstandings can cause whole applications to be rejected, wasting time and resources for the administrative agents and clients involved. As more rules are added, the cost and effort needed to check and document everything grow much faster than the rules themselves.

These observations motivate the growing interest in computational approaches to compliance. Encoding regulations in machine-interpretable form enables automated reasoning about norms for planning and negotiating, as well as systematic detection of violations and finding mitigation solutions.

\section{What are Formal Methods?}

Formal methods rest on a simple but demanding principle \cite{woodcock2009formal}. System descriptions and their expected behavior are treated as mathematical objects, and questions about their behavior are formulated as precise logical or algebraic problems. This shift increases the modeling effort, but it replaces persuasive arguments with verifiable outcomes such as theorems, counterexamples, or computed verdicts.

Formal methods could help guarantee correctness for the digitalization of normative systems by addressing a basic problem in modern regulation: the replacement of natural language and human interpretation with clear, formal language and automated, checkable rules for specifying and deciding. Closing this gap becomes increasingly important if we want to enforce rules reliably as systems get more complex.

Formal methods can be broadly classified by the nature of their inputs and the type of analysis they provide; we briefly outline the most relevant categories:

\paragraph{Model-based verification.}

Model-based verification, such as model checking, operates on symbolic representations of system behavior, such as transition systems, SAT encodings, and Petri nets, while the properties to check against are specified in logics or formal languages such as regular expressions and temporal logics \cite{DBLP:journals/cacm/ClarkeES09, DBLP:books/daglib/0020348}. The challenges for these methods are developing an expressive enough logic while remaining decidable for satisfiability and model checking, and designing the right abstraction to represent the system under study, in which behavior can be finitely represented or soundly abstracted to avoid the known problem of state explosion. 

\paragraph{Deductive and proof-based methods.}

Theorem-proving and proof-carrying approaches reason at the level of logical theories rather than at the level of state exploration. Systems are described by axioms, inference rules, and invariants. Correctness is based on proofs in a formal calculus, either purely manually or in a semi-automated way using fixpoints. They are designed to handle more complex systems, such as infinite-state systems and richer data domains than model-based verification, but on the other hand rely more on human guidance for appropriate abstraction choices, proof construction, invariant guessing, and false-positive alarm mitigation \cite{DBLP:books/sp/93/Hoare93,DBLP:books/ph/Dijkstra76,DBLP:conf/popl/CousotC77}.

\paragraph{Specification and system-contract methods.}

Specification languages, design-by-contract techniques, and normative formalisms focus on what a system must provide. The primary objects of analysis are system contracts with formal semantics, depending on the system's domain of application \cite{DBLP:phd/us/Romeo22,DBLP:journals/fteda/BenvenisteCNPRR18}.

\paragraph{Runtime and monitor-based methods.}

Runtime verification and monitoring techniques analyze behavior represented as traces of a system and/or its agents. Instead of establishing correctness for all possible behaviors, they evaluate concrete traces against a formal specification. The result may be a verdict that evolves over time, such as satisfaction, violation, or an inconclusive status. These methods sacrifice completeness for practicality and are effective when full system models are unavailable, too complex, or when systems operate in open or partially observable environments \cite{DBLP:journals/jlp/LeuckerS09,bartocci2018rv}.

Formal methods do not remove complexity, nor do they verify everything. They make the complexity explicit, structure it, and delimit precisely what can and cannot be correctly verified as a consequence of Rice's theorem \cite{rice1953classes}.

\section{Applications of Formal Methods in Computational Law}

The intersection of law and computer science has contributed to the development of \emph{Computational Law} \cite{love2005computational}. This field aims to formalize the representation and automated analysis of normative specifications such as laws, regulations, and contractual terms. The final goal is the construction of computational systems to reason about their application.

In this field, formal methods are used to close the gap between expressive natural languages, which are rich but often unclear, and automated analysis techniques that need to be clear and solvable. By using mathematical logic, formal methods create representations of rules that computers can process, allowing for clear interpretation, systematic checking, and repeatable reasoning. This makes it possible to analyze compliance even under real-world constraints such as time constraints, multiple actions occurring simultaneously, and distributed decision-making. These methods are now used in several areas:

\paragraph{Automating Compliance and Requirements.}

Formal methods enable computational systems to determine exactly which legal requirements apply to a specific situation. This includes determining obligations, prohibitions, and permissions, as well as detecting violations. Systems can automatically check if a set of facts violates a regulation and identify whether compensatory clauses have been satisfied \cite{governatori2018compliance}.

\paragraph{Business Process and Software Engineering.}

Formal methods integrate legal compliance into the design phase of technical systems. Organizations can verify if business process blueprints comply with regulations before deployment \cite{vanDerAalst2011compliance}.

\paragraph{Regulatory Analysis and Design.}

Regulators use formal tools to improve the quality of legislation. Digital versions of legislation allow agencies to identify inconsistencies, logical loops, or redundant conditions \cite{sergot1986british}.

\paragraph{Safety and Autonomous Systems.}

In real-time systems, formal methods encode complex rules for machine execution. For example, researchers have encoded overtaking clauses from Queensland road rules into logic to test how autonomous vehicles interpret safe distances and ``clear views'' in simulation \cite{DBLP:conf/jurix/BhuiyanGBDIR20}.

\section{Scope and Contributions of the Dissertation}

While the general application of formal methods to law is promising, significant challenges remain in handling the dynamic aspects of normative systems. This dissertation focuses specifically on the challenges of \emph{timed and distributed ought-to-do normative systems}.

Existing approaches often treat norms as static constraints or verify them centrally. However, real-world contracts, laws, and regulations involve multiple agents operating asynchronously, where the timing of actions (e.g., deadlines, durations) is as critical as the actions themselves.

Furthermore, in distributed settings, agents may have different or conflicting understandings of their normative state.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This dissertation studies the formal analysis of normative specifications within a deliberately limited-expressivity formal logic. The main goal is to develop preliminary logical frameworks aligned with the reasoning used in legal and regulatory practice, with a special focus on conflicts in metric-timed normative specifications and on blame assignment in collaborative multi-agent normative specifications. 
The expressiveness limitation keeps reasoning frameworks decidable, easy to understand, and transferable.

The main contributions of this work are:

 \begin{enumerate}[Con1]

   \item We introduce a logical framework that treats \emph{timed normative conflicts} as the primary objects of reasoning. The development is grounded in satisfiability checking, semantic-preserving syntactic transformations, and a dedicated conflict calculus. The framework also develops a quantitative measure of the degree of conflict within a normative specification and, finally, an automatic conflict-elimination procedure that preserves semantic equivalence whenever repair is possible. 

 \item We present a runtime verification framework that evaluates bilateral contract compliance. The main feature is to reason about and distinguish the \emph{attempt} to perform an action from its \emph{successful outcome}. This attempt-based interaction model allows the system to accurately assign responsibility for non-compliance, recognizing when a failure is caused by one agent's lack of effort versus the other agent's active interference or refusal to collaborate.

  \end{enumerate}

\section{Structure of the Dissertation}

This thesis is structured into the following four chapters:

\begin{description}
  \item[\ref{chapter:preliminaries}] defines the data structures and helper functions, mainly to represent time in interval sets and the different types of traces used as models for the developed logics. The goal is to ensure that later definitions and results can be stated clearly using these data structures in the contribution chapters. 
  \item[\ref{chap:conflict}] presents the static analysis of normative specifications under metric time and studies the formal notion of timed normative conflicts. The chapter defines when time-constrained obligations and prohibitions are incompatible and how such conflicts can be detected syntactically and characterized semantically. It further introduces measures and rewriting techniques that allow conflicting specifications to be analyzed and, when possible, repaired without altering their intended meaning.
  \item[\ref{chap:monitoring}] develops monitoring semantics and constructions for violation and blame that distinguish between individual agents' attempts and the joint outcomes of their collaborations during contract execution. Based on this distinction, it provides monitoring mechanisms that assign blame to agents during the full duration of contract enforcement for each case of non-compliance.
  \item[\ref{chapter:conclusion}] revisits the main technical contributions in light of the initial research questions and reflects on how to combine the results of the contributions as well as the limitations of the proposed approaches and their implications for future developments.
\end{description}
