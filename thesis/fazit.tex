\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{chapter:conclusion}

\section{Summary}
This dissertation explores key challenges in using formal methods to digitalize and automate the analysis of normative systems. As more laws, regulations, and contracts guide how autonomous software agents act, the vagueness of natural language becomes a challenge. To address this challenge, we demonstrate that Normative System Engineering can achieve practical efficacy by maintaining a controlled expressiveness in the underlying logic. By restricting complexity to ensure tractable reasoning, we can automate tasks such as conflict detection and blame attribution.

The first main contribution, covered in \ref{chap:conflict}, concerns the static analysis of normative specifications. We introduced Micro Metric-Time Normative Logic (\TDDLfm) to formalize the identification of conflicts between time-constrained obligations and prohibitions. We developed a special normal form, called Disjunctive Punctual Normal Form (\dpnf), to break down complex time rules into punctual, checkable parts while preserving key semantic properties such as the Lowest Upper Common Operator (\LUCO) and Literal Ancestor Preservation (\LAP). This setup facilitates detailed analysis of conflicts via pattern matching. We have also established a direct link between the conflicts found and Minimal Unsatisfiable Subsets (MUS). By fixing a constraint on metric traces, we could clearly distinguish deontic conflicts, i.e., normative contradictions, and more specifically, define ontic conflicts, i.e., an agent's inability to perform actions due to a time limitation. We also defined a measure of conflict density, linked it to unsatisfiability, and developed a procedure to remove it to obtain a conflict-free formula. We have also developed a conflict calculus to support the manual refinement of a normative specification. This framework is designed to help normative specification designers review and refine rules before deployment, ensuring they are clear and practical.

The second main contribution, discussed in \ref{chap:monitoring}, concerns the monitoring of collaborative interactions during contract enforcement. We developed Two-Agents Collaborative Normative Logic (\cDL) to model contracts where compliance depends on the joint actions of two parties. A key part of this framework is that it reasons about agents' attempts and collaboration outcomes. This distinction allows the monitors to correctly attribute blame to the right agents. To fully explain how interactions between violations and blames are propagated, we built a layered semantic framework.

First, we developed a forward-looking semantic family to detect exactly when the first satisfaction or violation happens, using deterministic Moore machines. We then moved beyond simple first satisfaction/violation verdicts by adding Quantitative Blame Semantics. To achieve this quantitative approach, the monitoring semantics tracks how contracts change with a contract progress function that persists beyond the first violation and separately counts the score using a second scoring function. By storing these score distributions as blame vectors for each agent, the framework provides a detailed view of responsibility, enabling it to explain each failure. Translating this reasoning into executable Mealy machines provides a reliable and practical way to track normative rules in real time and settle disputes fairly afterward.

Together, these chapters provide a preliminary and novel formal methodology for automating parts of normative systems.

\section{Future Work}
This dissertation explored new research directions in the theory of formal semantics for normative reasoning. Future work will combine the main methods of conflict-aware temporal reasoning and responsibility-sensitive collaboration into more expressive and practical frameworks. Key objectives include expanding these foundations to handle more complex timing, multi-agent relationships, and normative compliance. These extensions will bring normative logic closer to real-world normative systems and enable richer verification, automated monitoring, and accountability for such systems.

\subsection{Toward a Unified Formalism (\logic{MTTACNL})}
The next step for this research is to combine the two main formalisms developed in this dissertation. \ref{chap:conflict} introduced \TDDLfm, which focuses on metric-time aspects of normative specifications in a single-agent setting, whereas \ref{chap:monitoring} presented \cDL, which focuses on a two-agent setting with more operators and uses logical time. However, keeping them separate limits their use in situations that have both strict timing rules and cooperative dependencies. To bridge this gap, a primary objective for future work is to develop a unified framework, tentatively styled as the \emph{Metric-Timed Two-Agents Collaborative Normative Logic} (\logic{MTTACNL}).

Creating this unified framework will require a complete rebuild of the underlying semantic models. Specifically, the synchronous trace settings used in \cDL must be replaced by \emph{metric-timed traces}, in which every collaborative action and every attempt are associated with a precise real-valued timestamp, and collaboration attempts/blocking do not necessarily occur at the same time point. On the syntax side, the logic should adequately combine interval-based constraints with collaborative operators. This richer syntax allows specifying complex requirements, such as ``Agent 1 must accomplish action $\acta$ within 5 time units without interference from Agent 2.''

Putting this unified logic into practice will require a two-part approach to automated reasoning. First, for static analysis, the conflict detection algorithms from Chapter 3 need to be adapted to handle interference between multiple agents. This change would allow regulators to verify both that temporal windows remain feasible and that the contract does not force any agent into a position where they must actively interfere with another to fulfill their own duties. Second, for dynamic enforcement, the monitoring system needs to move from standard Moore machines to timed automata or other real-time computational models. Achieving this framework will provide more practical reasoning and a closer alignment with real-world multi-agent normative systems.

\subsection{Beyond Discrete Actions}
In this dissertation, we worked with the assumption that actions are atomic and instantaneous. While discrete actions do occur in practice and many real-world rules fit this model, many norms require actions that take time to succeed, not just in a single moment. A logical next step is to include proper reasoning for actions with durations, as suggested by reviewers at the JURIX conference.

Pushing this complexity further requires moving to the domain of \emph{continuous real-time actions}, a challenging counterpart to discrete logic, in which the system must monitor varying physical signals such as velocity or temperature rather than symbolic transitions. To handle this continuous domain, one needs to develop clear abstractions that connect normative reasoning with control theory.

Looking beyond the timing aspect of actions brings us to the key difference between operational steps and declarative goals. Our current formalisms are strictly ought-to-do logics, focusing only on the agents' transitions and interactions. However, many real-world regulations are naturally expressed as \emph{ought-to-be} specifications that constrain the resulting system state rather than the specific means of achieving it. For example, a safety regulation may mandate that a room remain below a certain temperature without dictating the specific actions to be taken to operate the cooling/heating system. Capturing the full spectrum of real-life normative specifications therefore requires a hybrid logic capable of reasoning about both the actions agents perform and the environmental states those actions induce.
